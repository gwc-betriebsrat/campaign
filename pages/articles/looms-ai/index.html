AI Age & the 150-year-old Contract

A story about labour rights. And the person who didn't know they had any, until they did.

Three moments, one pattern
If you still believe your employer can fire you, harass you, transfer you, change your hours, or put undue expectations on you at will, it is time to get educated.
In one paragraph

The rights you have at work in Germany weren't given to you. They were built by workers who refused to be disposable, written into law more than a century ago, and still apply to you the moment you sign your contract. But a Works Council can only use those rights if it's willing to. That's what the upcoming vote is for. This article is the longer story, if you want it.

In Germany, employment is not just a private contract between a powerful company and a replaceable employee. It is a social relationship between you, them, and the law workers fought for long before any of us arrived.

This article is about the new arrival. Someone who moved to Berlin for work. Who signed the contract they were handed. Who assumed the terms were standard, because everyone seemed to be working under the same ones.

They are not the reader. Not yet.

I. The weavers

A story about skill, and what happens when it becomes disposable.

TL;DR: Skill used to be leverage. Machines ended that. The worker became a cost to minimise, not a partner to keep.

Consider the Silesian weavers of the 1840s.

They had a skill. Built over years. That let them earn and carry with them as identity, not just income. That fed their families, anchored their standing in their communities, and gave their work meaning beyond the wage.

Before the machines, a weaver's skill was their leverage. An employer couldn't replace a trained weaver easily, so the relationship had terms: fair pay, standing in the community, apprenticeships passed down.

Then came mechanised looms. Their skill was commoditised to machines. What took a weaver weeks, a machine did in hours. Factory owners could train cheap labour in a day. The skilled weavers were kept only where they were cheap enough to justify, paid only enough to survive the month, and disposable the moment the arithmetic changed. This was the capitalistic arithmetic — indifferent to the fair pay that would have let the same workers build an independent livelihood, even as they built multi-million and billion-dollar industries.

The wealth those factories produced was built on these very workers. When the technology shifted, the workers were the ones discarded. The owners kept the gains. The workers kept the hunger.

This is the injustice the labour movement named. Unacceptable then. Unacceptable now. The law was built to prevent it, not out of charity, but out of recognition that a system which treats people as variable inputs will eventually produce the economic destitution of the worker. When the salary stops, there is nothing left in the pocket to pay for housing and food. The balance of a worker's life was already on edge. Something had to hold the weight.

II. Bismarck's arithmetic

Why the most conservative chancellor in Europe built the first welfare state.

TL;DR: Worker protections aren't charity. They're the cheapest way to prevent the response destitution creates. Works councils came from the same logic.

In the 1880s, Otto von Bismarck introduced the world's first comprehensive social welfare system. Health insurance. Accident insurance. Old-age pensions. Not because he cared about the working class. He didn't.

He built it because he understood a simple equation. A population with nothing left to lose does not stay peaceful. Revolution, in the end, is cheaper to prevent than to survive.

Worker protections were never gifts. They were concessions to prevent economic destitution from becoming revolution.

A generation later, after the First World War, works councils were written into German law. The Betriebsrat. Not as a favour, but as structural recognition that democracy in the streets without democracy in the workplace, where workers spent most of their waking lives, was not stable. Codetermination was the answer: the legal right for workers to have a seat at the table where decisions about their working conditions are made.

This is the inheritance. Built on the backs of people who refused to accept that economic vulnerability was a fair price for a wage.

III. The same moment

We tell ourselves we live in different times.

TL;DR: AI is today's loom. The logic is identical: skill commoditised, worker disposable. For expats, the cost of speaking up is even higher.

We don't send children into coal mines. We don't depend on a hand loom for dinner. The 19th century feels resolved. Historical. Not relevant to us.

But look around:

  • Mass layoffs announced each quarter, attributed to AI.
  • "Restructuring" used as language for dressing up OpEx and CapEx for the market.
  • Performance frameworks that measure workers against targets that move.
  • Burnout treated as a personal failure rather than a structural one.
  • The quiet expectation of being reachable in the evenings, because refusing marks you as uncommitted.

And for those whose residency depends on the job, the cost of speaking up feels higher than the cost of silence. Which is exactly how silence is produced.

The uniforms are different. The language is different. The outcome is familiar.

This is not a different moment. It is the same moment, with a laptop instead of a loom.

The skill that took years to build is being commoditised in months. The worker is being asked, once again, to accept that their livelihood is negotiable. That technological progress means some of them become disposable. That speaking up carries professional risk. That pushing back marks them as "not a team player."

This is the exact logic the weavers faced. The exact logic Bismarck recognised as politically dangerous, capable of creating a revolution. The tools built in response — codetermination, works councils, the legal right to challenge employer decisions — still exist, still written into German law.

The only question is whether anyone at the table is willing to use them.

IV. The realisation

Somewhere along the way, the new arrival started to notice.

TL;DR: The rights were always there. §87, §99, §80(2), the EU pay directive. Unused, because nobody said they existed and nobody at the table picked them up.

That the terms they accepted were not universal. That employment in Germany was not the transaction they'd been trained to expect. That what looked like a private contract was, in fact, a three-way relationship: the company, the worker, and the law.

They noticed that the colleagues who understood this had a different relationship with their work. Less anxious. More grounded. They pushed back on unreasonable requests not because they were brave, but because they knew the law was on their side.

They noticed that the Works Council existed. That it had real legal power. That it was supposed to represent them. And that somehow, nothing about their working life suggested anyone was actually using that power on their behalf.

They started to learn what codetermination actually looks like in practice. When employees have faced sudden role changes, a willing Works Council can refuse consent under §99 until individual cases are reviewed properly. When management has proposed restructurings, the law requires a negotiated social plan, not a take-it-or-leave-it exit. When working hours or surveillance practices shift, §87 means those changes cannot be rolled out unilaterally. The rights are not symbolic. They are operational.

They started reading. They learned about §87 (codetermination over working hours). §99 (consent required for transfers). §80(2) (the right to request salary data). The EU pay transparency directive arriving in 2026/2027.

They realised, slowly, that the rights had been there the whole time. Someone had fought for them. Someone had written them into law. They had simply been sitting there, unused, because nobody had told the new arrival they existed, and nobody at the table had been willing to pick them up.

And then the sharper realisation. Electing a Works Council is not just electing names. It is electing whether the law gets used — or whether it sits at the table as a reminder nobody acts on.

V. The vote

The new arrival was never just a bystander.

TL;DR: GWC members lived these patterns firsthand. The rights apply to you from the moment you signed. The question is whether the people you elect know how to use them.

Members of our list have faced this vulnerability firsthand. A transfer without warning. A performance rating disconnected from the work. A role that shifted while no one consulted us. A "restructuring" conversation that started and ended without us in the room.

We are not standing because we read about these patterns. We are standing because we lived them. And because we learned that the tools exist. The failure was not in the law, but in whether anyone at the table was willing to use it — or let it slide with a strongly-worded reminder to the employer.

What this means in practice. We will demand individual review of every role change, not standardised exits. We will request the salary data §80(2) guarantees and use it to challenge unequal pay. We will insist that major decisions reach the workforce before they are finalised, not after. We will document what happens in co-determination meetings, so that accountability is not a promise but a practice. This is what "willing" looks like.

The rights you have access to were built by people who refused to accept economic vulnerability as the price of a job. They are written into German law. They do not care whether you were born here or arrived last year. They apply to you the moment you signed your contract.

The only question left is whether you use them, and whether the people you elect to represent you know how.
