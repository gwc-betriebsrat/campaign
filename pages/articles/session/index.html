Inside the conversation: AI, layoffs, transparency, and what a Works Council actually does

On May 4th, Global Workforce Community hosted a panel called Managing Digital Transformation Responsibly. The conversation brought together a labour researcher, a Berlin tech organiser, and a current Zalando Works Council member. This recap is an early preview of the content, with more to follow.

Across Germany, Europe, and the wider world, economic restructuring driven by AI and automation is reshaping entire industries. The change is real, the pace is fast, and the cost lands on employees first. This is already happening.

Many of us heard the story of Erfurt, Zalando's first warehouse, where colleagues with the longest tenure saw their roles rewritten by automation arriving in Giessen on the other side of Germany. Similar pressures are now landing in tech. The question is no longer whether they will affect us, but who will be at the table when they do.

This panel is part of an ongoing series of informative events Global Workforce Community has been hosting, and will continue to host, on topics that affect work life at Zalando. Our view is that everything starts with awareness, and we will keep investing in employee education long past this election cycle. The May 4th panel was hosted by Daniela Nigro and ran about 45 minutes.

Who was in the conversation
Yonatan Miller
Software engineer and trade union organiser, originally from New York, now based in Berlin. Co-founder of Berlin Tech Workers Coalition and a ver.di trade union organiser. Supports the formation of new Works Councils in tech and mentors them afterwards.
Valentin Niebler
Doctoral candidate at the Institute for European Ethnology and the Berlin Institute for Migration Studies, Humboldt University. Research focus: conflict resolution and regulation in the tech industry.
Daniel Calderon
Application Security Engineer in Information Security at Zalando, where he has worked for over 7.5 years. Member of Zalando SE Berlin Works Council since 2022, currently full-time. Elected as official assessor for the 2026 reorganization negotiations.
TL;DR — five things that came up
  • Zalando was an early adopter of a Works Council in tech (2020), but other Berlin tech companies have since pushed further on transparency, agreements, and codetermination practice.
  • For Berlin's heavily international workforce, layoffs carry extra weight: residency, family relocation, and visa status. German law does not list migration as a protected vulnerable status, but Works Councils can negotiate it into social plans.
  • Works Agreements are the legal documents that regulate company-wide rules (evaluations, time tracking, working hours, surveillance, AI tools, benefits). Zalando SE Berlin currently has five.
  • The current Works Council reviewed and approved tools like Google Gemini. Daniel said the work is about adopting AI without losing employee privacy, dignity, and job security in the process — not blocking adoption.
  • The recent 2026 reorganization negotiations went into formal arbitration. The outcome included a voluntary component (allowing some employees to leave on good terms in place of those otherwise selected for dismissal), a fair severance package, and a block on targeted dismissals.
Topic 1 · Transparency

How does Zalando look from outside the company?

Yonatan opened with a direct take. Zalando was one of the earliest tech companies in Berlin to form a Works Council, in 2020. Since then, around 30 other tech workplaces in Berlin have followed. At network-wide conferences of tech Works Councils organised in 2022 and 2023, Zalando has not been visibly part of the public conversation. Several Zalando Works Council members showed up at those meetings expressing frustration about how things were being handled internally.

"It was an early adopter, but perhaps because it was an early adopter, it didn't have a reference point what to compare it to."

— Yonatan Miller

A Works Council is a toolbox, Yonatan said. Many of the tools are optional. Whether they get used depends on the people in the seats and on whether they network with other Works Councils to learn what is being done elsewhere.

Topic 2 · Layoffs and the migrant workforce

What protections exist, and what gaps the law leaves open

Yonatan described two ways layoffs typically happen in tech companies. The first is a transparent process: management proposes a percentage, the Works Council pushes back, and the workforce is informed early enough to make plans. The second is opaque, where even affected employees may not know how many others are affected or what the terms are. For Zalando's heavily international workforce, this distinction carries extra weight: many colleagues are on visa-dependent contracts, and even those who aren't face higher costs of relocation.

German law gives special vulnerable status to people with disabilities, parents, and older workers. Migration is not on that protected list. At his previous workplace, Yonatan said, the Works Council closed that gap deliberately, by negotiating extended notice periods for colleagues with migrant status (at half pay if needed) into the social plan, and by treating all visa types equally.

"This only happens when you network with other Works Councils and industry experts."

— Yonatan Miller

Valentin reinforced this from a research angle. Tech workers in Berlin often arrive into what he called a "honeymoon phase": free fruit, flat hierarchies, the language of family and creativity. After a few years, the same patterns surface across companies, and workers start to recognise their dependency on management decisions even when the culture frames them as family or creatives.

Topic 3 · Works Agreements

The legal documents most employees never see

Daniel introduced what he called the most underused part of the Works Council role. A Works Agreement (Betriebsvereinbarung) is a binding legal document between the employer and the Works Council that regulates internal company matters affecting all employees: evaluations, time tracking, working hours, surveillance, AI tools, benefits, and more.

Daniel noted that Zalando SE Berlin currently has five Works Agreements, while the topics that could be covered run into the dozens or higher. Yonatan added that an extensive informal collection of agreements exists across the Berlin tech network, used for benchmarking how comprehensive different companies' protections actually are.

Topic 4 · AI adoption and codetermination

What role should the Works Council play when new tools are introduced?

Daniel described the Works Council's stance on AI as enabling adoption within a framework that protects employees, rather than blocking it. Google Gemini, which many Zalando colleagues now use, went through Works Council review and was approved. Many tools have. The codetermination work is about scrutinizing how each tool is deployed and whether it preserves employee privacy, dignity, and job security.

The harder question, Daniel said, comes when AI is used to justify replacing roles. He cited Klarna and Duolingo, both of which announced replacing teams with AI and later walked the decisions back when the work didn't hold up. The cost of those reversals fell on the people who had already been laid off.

"Employee privacy, dignity, and job security really need to be thought of here."

— Daniel Calderon

A Works Council that is involved early enough can shape these decisions before they happen. Once they are made, the Works Council can negotiate severance, alternatives, and retraining, but the harm to the affected colleagues is already in motion. Valentin added that AI is sometimes used by management as a placeholder for "you have to adjust without concessions" — a Works Council with the right framework is what turns that conversation into a real one about what adaptation looks like and who pays for the transition.

Topic 5 · The 2026 reorganization at Zalando

What happened in the recent negotiations

Daniel gave the clearest publicly shared description of recent reorganization negotiations to date. The proposal affected roles in the range of 150 to 200 jobs. The Works Council attempted to negotiate, and when those negotiations did not progress further, the matter went into formal arbitration through what is called an Einigungsstelle: a process where a judge sits between management and the Works Council, with three assessors elected from each side. Daniel was elected as one of the Works Council assessors.

The outcome included three things he highlighted as wins: a voluntary component, where a subset of employees are offered the option to leave of their own free will and on good terms, saving the place of someone selected for dismissal under the social plan; a fair severance package negotiated as part of the social plan; and a block on targeted dismissals, which had appeared in management's initial proposal.

Whether the legal tools get used in any given negotiation depends on whether the Works Council is prepared, has expert support, and is willing to push the matter into formal arbitration when an agreement cannot be reached at the table.

Closing thoughts

What ties the three perspectives together

Across all three guests, one observation kept resurfacing: a Works Council in Germany has substantial legal power, but using that power is not automatic. Many of the codetermination tools are optional in practice, even when their scope is broad in law. What shapes the lived experience of employees over the next four years is whether those tools get exercised.

"The Works Council is not the end goal. It's the starting point."

— Yonatan Miller

Valentin closed by noting that the international diversity of tech workforces is both an advantage and a vulnerability, and that he saw the focus on this as one of GWC's distinctive strengths.
