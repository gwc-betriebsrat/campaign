The Power Gap Is Costing You. Right Now.

When the legal tools exist but nobody uses them, you lose. We see this pattern in critical issues which affect employees, including two playing out right now.

The Performance Framework: Built Without Transparency, Input, or Guardrails

Your performance rating may not reflect your work. It may reflect a distribution target the performance framework never officially intended to apply.

On paper, you are evaluated against three official criteria: Role Expectations, OFM & Focus Areas. In practice, unofficial distribution targets can override all three, working their way down through management. The top outcomes (Leading Ahead & Delivering Breakthrough) become quotas with limited seats, regardless of how many people actually earned them. In some cases, if a manager's team has no one flagged as underperforming, the manager risks being told they aren't evaluating rigorously enough. And there's no mechanism for you or your lead to challenge this.

And for some, the imposed effects ripple beyond your rating into your compensation and growth opportunities. And when roles are filled without transparent and fair evaluation of internal candidates, growth starts being a dead end.

"When you feed a system with top-down distribution targets and cap the number of people who can be rated as excelling, you get systemic unfairness and unaddressed pay gaps as a result. This process is working for leadership, but it is failing our teams." — A first-line Engineering Manager

Under German law, an employer cannot implement or change a performance framework without the Works Council's explicit consent. You would have been asked for input. Your perspective would have shaped the terms. That didn't happen.

An official Works Agreement should set guardrails to protect against this kind of misuse with unofficial quotas to undermine your rating, give you a way to challenge unfair outcomes, and ensure that PSPs cannot be used as termination pipelines. Legal leverage for this has barely been touched.

The fundamental question remains: where is the official Works Agreement that should govern this process? And why were employees only informed by the employer after the terms were set?

Reorganizations: What You Don't Know Is Costing You

You've lived through restructurings. But do you know what happens on the other side of the table?

When some colleagues describe a culture of fear and instability around restructurings, there's a story behind that emotion.

110 reorganizations processed last year. Over 2 per week.

Weekly restructurings don't allow rigorous case-by-case review, codetermination of transfers, or independent arbitration. The legal standard is structurally impossible to meet.

Passively or actively: how the Works Council handles a restructuring directly determines whether alternative opportunities were genuinely explored, whether affected colleagues were given a fair chance at other roles, and what severance terms are negotiated for those who leave. The legal tools exist. The question is whether anyone at the table is using them.

For colleagues whose residency depends on their employment, or who are supporting families in one of Europe's most expensive cities, the cost of that question going unanswered is existential.

As this term comes to an end, the reorganization pattern is being disrupted. Where previous rounds followed the standard process, the negotiating team behind this reorg connected the dots between these rolling restructurings. And by insisting on binding standards for severance and job security, the process has been escalated to an independent conciliation committee.

It matters who sits at the negotiating table. Legal leverage works when representatives are willing to use the tools provided by the law. Imagine what becomes possible when that willingness has the backing of your vote.

When the legal tools close the gap

What Changes for the Workforce

Legal standard. Competence. Transparency. And the will to prioritize your protection over management comfort.

Job Protection & Radical Support

"When I got the PSP, I didn't know who to turn to. My manager said it was their decision. HR said it was a standard process. Nobody mentioned I had rights and that a Works Council could be involved at all."

Unshakeable support in the era of AI, automation, and platform shifts. No employee should face a PSP, a termination, or discrimination without us in their corner. We use legal tools to end dismissals that lack proper justification and process and provide unwavering defense, regardless of your contract type, background, or department.

Fair Compensation & Transparent Growth

"I've been told my salary is 'competitive,' but my experience and my peers suggest different. Even high performers stay stuck below their midpoint after years of excellence."

With salary bands, levels, and midpoints, on paper the system looks objective. In practice, quotas distort your ratings in ways that have nothing to do with your work. There is no meaningful appeal when the system fails you. Works Agreements need to establish guaranteed appeal rights, guardrail criteria, transparency on how rating decisions are made, and visible salary bands across levels so that "competitive pay" is something you can verify, not just accept. When evaluation rounds don't reward excellence, the framework has failed you, not the other way around. An Internal Mobility Agreement would ensure that internal talent is genuinely invested in before looking outward, and that growth paths don't quietly close while new roles open for others.

Stable Flexibility

"Our remote work policy changed overnight with a Google Chat message. No consultation, no discussion."

Your flexibility isn't a perk; it allows you to be a parent, a caregiver, a person with a life beyond the office. There's a Works Agreement for mobile work, the only one delivered by the previous Works Council. Most employees don't even know it exists. And "Work from abroad"? No such formal agreement exists at all. Both need to be strengthened, made visible, and protected, alongside flexible hours, so your arrangements can't be revoked with a policy update. In Germany, bank holidays that fall on weekends are simply lost. Willing Works Councils have secured this through work agreements. We can too. Your flexibility deserves favourable agreements, not a revocable favour.

Workload & Sustainability

"After each round of restructuring, the people who stay inherit the work of those who left. Teams shrink but expectations don't. And when we have inputs, somehow we are 'lacking resilience.'"

When teams are understaffed and the workload doubles overnight, that's not growth opportunity and team efficiency. It's a system failing the people who stayed. We will advocate for workload protections, meaningful support during transitions, and the right to raise concerns without being labeled as lacking resilience. Sustainable performance requires sustainable conditions, and the Council has the right and the duty to address them.

Accountability & Transparency

"Every quarter, zBeat results confirm what we already know, but nothing structurally changes. It feels like shouting into a system designed to listen but not to act. Is anyone with the power even willing to be accountable to fix problems beyond managing us?"

This is exactly where Works Council members with the will to use the full legal leverage can make a change. The council has the legal right to access employee data like zBeat results, hold management accountable for acting on systemic issues, and ensure that transparency isn't optional. When feedback leads to binding agreements instead of internal presentations, the cycle of "acknowledge and move on" ends. Your voice stops being a data point and starts becoming a mandate.
