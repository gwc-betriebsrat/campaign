The Pay Gap Isn't a Mystery.
The Tools to Fix It Aren't Either.

Germany gives you the legal right to see your peer median salary. A Works Council with the will to act can go further. Here's what both look like in practice. And why the next election term is when it gets real.

In Germany, the gender pay gap has a legal answer. You don't have to rely on office rumours. The Entgelttransparenzgesetz (Germany's Pay Transparency Act), created specifically to close the gender pay gap, gives you the legal right to see the median pay for peers of the opposite gender doing comparable work.

I'm Below My Peer Median Salary. Now What?

Finding out you're below the median isn't automatically a sign of trouble. Recent promotions and time in role are common explanations. You'll often start at the lower end of a new grade while you build tenure.

But these explanations don't cover every case. Each performance outcome comes with a salary adjustment range. Your lead has discretion over where you land within that range. Who gets placed at the higher end? Often the person who pushes hardest. Who stays at the lower end? Often the person who doesn't push, or isn't expected to.

Colleagues returning from parental leave or sabbaticals have the same right to participate in evaluations. But in some organisations, their budget allocation is treated as available to redistribute, as if being away means you should expect less.

And if your rating itself was shaped by unofficial quotas on outcome levels from top, rather than individual merit alone, the distortion starts before your lead even touches the salary range.

When these patterns repeat across rounds, they compound. Same level, same outcome, same years. One person is consistently placed lower. Over time, the gap isn't about performance. It's about who the system rewards for negotiating and who it quietly underpays for not.

The transparency tool gives you the number. A Works Council with the will to act questions the system that produced it.

Can We Even Fix the Gap?

The Pay Transparency Act gives you the right to ask. But the Works Council has a broader right that most employees are never actively informed about.

§80(2) Works Constitution Act (BetrVG)

The Works Council can already request the data.

Under §80(2), the Works Council can request anonymized gross salary lists from the employer, not the company's finalized gender pay gap report, but the underlying data. This means a willing council doesn't have to wait for the company to shape what gets reported. It can independently assess where pay gaps exist, how they correlate with ratings and tenure, and whether those explanations are accurate.

This right exists today. It has been sitting unused. We will change that.

What's Coming: The EU Pay Transparency Directive

Starting 2026/2027, new EU rules shift what companies are legally required to do.

5%
If the gender pay gap exceeds 5% and cannot be justified by objective criteria, the company must conduct a joint pay assessment, together with the Works Council.

Companies with 250+ employees must report their gender pay gap annually. If the gap exceeds 5%, the company must conduct a joint pay assessment together with the Works Council. Not a presentation. Not a report filed unilaterally. A joint assessment where the council sits at the table with full access to the data.

This directive falls entirely within the next Works Council term (2026–2030). The people you elect in May will be the ones conducting these assessments, reviewing the data, and deciding whether gaps are fixed in practice or explained away on paper.

What a Willing Council Does With This

A Works Council serious about pay equity doesn't wait for the directive. It requests the salary data under §80(2) now, analyses it independently, and uses the findings when negotiating the next compensation round with management. The council has codetermination rights here. That is the leverage.

When the EU directive kicks in, a willing Works Council arrives prepared: with baseline data and identified gaps. One that didn't prepare loses the entire term catching up.

Knowing your number is your right. But transparency without enforcement changes nothing. A Works Council that acts can demand answers about why you stand where you stand and ensure the system producing those numbers is actually fair, not just reported on.

The tools exist and the directive is coming. On voting day, the question is who at the table intends to use them.
