Standardized Reorganizations
What happened between December and April

A one-size-fits-all doesn't work with shoes. It won't work with your future.

In December 2025, a draft Works Agreement was moving through the Works Council that would have fast-tracked reorganizations by creating a one-size-fits-all template. It didn't pass. This is what happened next, and what it means for you.

The December Fork

Reorganizations are a reality of professional life. But a restructuring done properly is built on high-quality, legally sound negotiations, rather than administrative shortcuts and simplifications.

Zalando's 2026 Reorganization process moved to a Negotiation Table chaired by a neutral external Judge (named as Einigungsstelle, the Conciliation Committee).

This isn't a setback. It is your rights being taken seriously.

It ends the era of the 'Internal Shortcut' and gives us maximum negotiating leverage. For the workforce, this means closing the loopholes on social protections, in compliance with labour law, that were otherwise easy to circumvent and ignore. It unlocks a path to higher severance packages, better transfer options, and rigorous scrutiny for every single layoff. It ensures your future is decided by the full weight of the law, not a corporate shortcut.

This restores a balance of power that was nearly sacrificed in December 2025. Despite an internal push to move forward with a 'fast-track' approach, the strategy was successfully steered back toward the full weight of the law, ensuring your future remains a negotiation, not just an exit with hardcoded standards.

I. The Balance at the Negotiating Table

Two tools that turn a reorganization into a real negotiation.

In German labor law, the Works Council relies on two engagement mechanisms to reach high-quality, equitable agreements during reorganizations: rigorous scrutiny and escalation.

A reorganization affecting livelihoods cannot be rushed. The law gives the Council the power to hit the brakes and demand to see the receipts, ensuring no one is fired just because it's cheaper for the company. The council can rigorously analyze and verify legal justifications, assess social impact, and negotiate on the plans before approval. Without this thorough due diligence, the employer could simply optimize for speed and bypass the interests of employees in favor of cheaper, faster, substandard solutions that don't respect what the labour laws of Germany intended to safeguard.

§99 BetrVG

Every individual transfer requires the Works Council's consent.

The employer cannot reassign, regrade, or transfer you to a new role without first obtaining the Works Council's formal consent. This isn't consultation. The council has the right to withhold approval when the transfer lacks proper justification.

When negotiations hit a wall, a party can escalate to the Conciliation Committee (Einigungsstelle), bringing in an external, neutral arbitrator, usually a judge. The possibility of an outside arbitrator deciding the final outcome creates a powerful incentive for both parties to find a superior agreement.

§76 BetrVG

Either side can escalate to an external judge.

If the Works Council and the employer cannot agree, either party can call for a Conciliation Committee, chaired by an external, neutral judge. The committee's decision is binding. This is not a last resort. It is the safety net that ensures neither side can simply wait out the other.

II. The Fast-Track Trap

What almost happened in December.

We are utilizing these powerful tools today, but we almost lost them entirely.

In December 2025, a proposed Works Agreement was moving through the Council. A Works Agreement is a binding contract that is supposed to secure rights above and beyond standard labor law.

A Works Agreement (Betriebsvereinbarung) is a formal, binding contract between the Works Council and the employer. Examples include preventing the company from using IT systems to monitor your activity, or establishing frameworks for performance evaluations. Publishing these is legally mandatory, and consulting employees before finalising them is often a standard part of the process.

But the goal of this specific agreement was to create a 'fast-track' template for reorganizations.

To a normal employee, "fast-tracking" a reorg might sound appealing. Nobody likes waiting in limbo. But in the context of labor law, "fast and standardized" means rushed. It ignores individual circumstances and leaves employees vulnerable to inequitable outcomes. By standardizing the process, this draft would have forced the Works Council to surrender your legal leverages, allowing the employer to bypass the deep negotiations the Works Council uses to get you the best job protections, transitions, and severances.

III. The Assembly Q&A vs. The Reality

What was said onstage. And what the facts suggested.

When the status of this proposed Works Agreement was raised at the March '26 Assembly, the response from some Works Council members onstage was that it was merely an employer idea, that no negotiations were happening, and that it would have no impact on your given rights.

However, the intense push to get a template ready by the end of 2025 told a very different story.

What was said

An employer idea. No negotiations happening. No impact on your given rights.

What the facts suggested

An intense push to get a template ready by the end of 2025, consistent with an effort to actively align on details.

When leadership claims no negotiations were happening while the facts point toward a rushed, year-end timeline, it creates a crisis of trust. Whether a draft is final or not misses the point. The issue is that the workforce is kept in the dark while the groundwork is being laid.

When the official narrative downplays the structural reality of these preparations, it creates a transparency gap that leaves the workforce uninformed. True representation isn't about managing the narrative or minimizing the facts. It is about consulting the workforce before their future is fast-tracked with a deadline. Anything less creates a critical gap in the representation you were promised.

IV. The GWC Standard

Why holding the line led to better results.

As representatives committed to radical transparency, we must set the record straight.

Contrary to those claims, our findings say this "voluntary" Works Agreement was not harmless. It was an attempt to fast-track reorganizations by creating a one-size-fits-all, time-boxed template that avoided triggering legal requirements to involve the Works Council more substantially.

Without principled resistance from some members within the Council, the draft would have moved toward approval without the public consultation the workforce deserved, especially where prior consultation is the norm in Germany.

By refusing to agree to that "voluntary agreement" at the end of 2025 without leverage, and instead securing legal counsel to properly negotiate the entire upcoming reorganization, dissenting members recognized a fundamental truth: without maintaining your baseline rights and legal leverage at the negotiating table, the employer has little reason to offer more than a basic package.

This institutional direction isn't just an isolated incident. It reflects a systemic pattern where speed is prioritized over employee involvement and legal leverage. For too long, the workforce has been excluded from critical decisions made on their behalf.

The December draft was simply an attempt to formalize that high-speed exclusion into a 'fast-track' template, effectively replacing deep legal scrutiny with a simplified, time-boxed process.

§111 BetrVG

Economic decisions affecting employees require formal consultation.

For operational changes that affect a substantial number of employees, the Works Council must be informed in full and in good time, and must be given the opportunity to negotiate a Reconciliation of Interests (Interessenausgleich) and a Social Plan (Sozialplan). A standardized, time-boxed template undermines this legal standard by design.

What we stand for

We are highly committed to diplomatic, constructive, formal cooperation with the employer. However, diplomacy is not about sacrificing quality for frictionless speed. It is about using the legal framework to ensure that the employer's need for efficiency is balanced by respect for your labor's contribution to our collective success, as recognized by the law.

Because members of this list and other Works Council members held the line, because they consulted and listened to legal experts, your protections are now being carefully, thoughtfully, and diligently negotiated with the full weight of the law, focused on finding the best possible deals and alternative dismissal preventions, instead of just a standardized exit hastily negotiated over the December holidays.
